### Teach a small, fixed set of comprehension strategies — explicitly

- **Evidence strength:** Strong (high impact; evidence rated moderate) · **Source:** EEF — Reading comprehension strategies (184 studies) · **Effect:** ≈ +7 months · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Reading comprehension strategies are one of the highest-impact, best-value approaches in the EEF Toolkit (+7 months). The gain comes from students learning a *small* set of strategies — predicting, questioning, clarifying, summarizing — well enough to use on their own. EEF notes this is a change to teaching habits that has to be sustained; dabbling doesn't move it.

**The move.** The AI generates slides and printable prompt cards for a few named strategies and reuses the *same* set every week until they're automatic.

**Worked example.**
> **While you read, keep asking:**
> • *Predict* — what do I think comes next, and what makes me think so?
> • *Clarify* — what's one word or sentence I should reread?
> • *Summarize* — what's the gist so far, in one sentence?

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* three or four strategies, reused until students do them without the card. *Badly:* a new strategy each week, or a card students fill in to hand to the teacher.

**Watch out.** More strategies isn't better — the impact is in the reps, not the variety.

### Run group reading with reciprocal-teaching roles

- **Evidence strength:** Strong · **Source:** Palincsar & Brown (1984); sits inside the EEF reading-comprehension entry · **Effect:** large comprehension gains in the original studies · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** In reciprocal teaching, small groups take turns leading four moves — predict, question, clarify, summarize — over a shared text. The original studies showed large comprehension gains. The effect depends on students eventually *running it themselves*: the teacher models first, then hands it over.

**The move.** The AI produces a printable set of four role cards, a one-page how-to, and a teacher script for the first few sessions that fades as groups take over.

**Worked example.**
> **Questioner card.** After your group reads the section, ask two questions: one whose answer is right there in the text, and one that makes the group think past it ("why do you think she lied?"). Call on two different people.

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* modeled a few times, then student-run. *Badly:* the teacher leads every turn — it becomes a read-aloud worksheet and the gains disappear.

**Watch out.** Roles need real modeling before students can hold them; don't hand out the cards cold.

### Make students talk about the text, not just read it

- **Evidence strength:** Strong (high impact) · **Source:** EEF — Oral language interventions · **Effect:** ≈ +6 months · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Structured talk about a text raises comprehension (+6 months for oral-language approaches). The effect is strongest when the talk pushes students to reason and cite evidence, not just recall what happened.

**The move.** The AI writes discussion-prompt sets that move past recall to inference and evidence, with a think-pair-share structure built in so every student talks once at low stakes first.

**Worked example.**
> **Think** (1 min, on your own): Was the narrator fair to her sister? Find one line that backs you up.
> **Pair:** trade your lines. Did you choose the same moment?
> **Share:** any pair want to name a line that changed their mind?

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* prompts with several defensible answers; everyone rehearses in a pair first. *Badly:* one-right-answer questions, or jumping straight to a whole-class hand-raise.

**Watch out.** Single-answer prompts kill discussion — favor questions people can genuinely disagree on.

### Front-load the knowledge and vocabulary a text assumes

- **Evidence strength:** Moderate–strong · **Source:** Recht & Leslie (1988); Willingham · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Comprehension is gated by background knowledge. In Recht & Leslie's well-known study, weaker readers who knew baseball out-comprehended stronger readers who didn't — the knowledge mattered more than the measured reading level. A text is only as readable as the knowledge it quietly assumes.

**The move.** Before a hard text, the AI generates a short "what you'll want to know first" pre-read: three or four key terms and one paragraph of background, aimed at exactly what the text takes for granted.

**Worked example.**
> **Before you read — three things that'll help:**
> *Rationing* = limits on how much you can buy. In the war, families got coupons for sugar and gas. Keep that in mind when the family fights at dinner — that's what they're really arguing about.

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* short, targeted at what the text assumes. *Badly:* a two-page summary that quietly replaces the reading.

**Watch out.** Keep it to what's needed for *this* text; a pre-read that does the comprehension for them defeats the point.

## Handle with care (for this goal)

- **Round-robin / "popcorn" reading.** Little comprehension benefit, raises anxiety, and most students disengage waiting their turn. The AI won't design materials around it.
- **Minutes-only reading logs.** Weak without a strategy or a real reason to read; can turn reading into a chore (see the overjustification note in `reducing-missing-work.md`).
- **Matching texts to "learning styles."** No support — see the handle-with-care list in `evidence-engine.md`.
