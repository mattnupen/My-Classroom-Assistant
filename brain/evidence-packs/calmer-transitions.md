# Evidence Pack — Calmer Transitions & Fewer Disruptions

**Goal:** Smoother starts, stops, and switches; less low-level disruption.
**Built:** 2026-06-14 · **Built by:** the evidence engine (see `brain/evidence-engine.md`)
**Sources checked:** EEF Behaviour interventions; classroom-management research (routines, precorrection, behavior-specific praise); channel factors (Leventhal, Singer & Jones, 1965).

### Teach the routine like content — model it, practice it

- **Evidence strength:** Strong (consistent classroom-management finding) · **Source:** PBIS classroom practices; classroom-management research · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Explicitly teaching, modeling, and practicing a transition routine — then reinforcing it — reliably cuts lost time and disruption. The active ingredient is sameness: the routine has to run the same way every day until it's automatic.

**The move.** The AI generates a step-numbered one-pager and a matching slide for the specific rocky moment (entry, cleanup, line-up).

**Worked example.**
> **Entry — first 3 minutes, every day:**
> 1. Phone in the caddy.
> 2. Warm-up's on the board — start it.
> 3. Pencil moving before the timer hits zero.

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* identical steps every day, posted and visible. *Badly:* redesigned each week, or explained once and assumed.

**Watch out.** Stability *is* the intervention — don't keep changing the steps.

### Pre-correct right before the hard moment

- **Evidence strength:** Moderate–strong · **Source:** precorrection research (Colvin; Sugai) · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** A brief, calm reminder of the expectation delivered *just before* a predictable trouble spot — not after the problem starts — reduces disruption. It works because it's preventive and neutral, not a reaction to something already going wrong.

**The move.** The AI writes a short pre-correction line into slide notes for the moment before the transition.

**Worked example.**
> [Before the switch to stations: "In one minute we move. That looks like: stand, push your chair in, walk to your number. Here we go."]

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* a quiet heads-up before, neutral tone. *Badly:* a warning barked after it's already loud.

**Watch out.** Pre-correction is a reminder, not a threat — the tone decides whether it works.

### Keep positives well ahead of corrections

- **Evidence strength:** Strong · **Source:** behavior-specific praise research; high positive-to-corrective ratios (often cited ~4:1–5:1) · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Classrooms that run a high ratio of specific positive to corrective interactions (commonly cited around 4:1 to 5:1) see better behavior and a warmer climate. *Specific* is what carries it — naming the action beats a generic "good job."

**The move.** The AI drafts specific, true praise lines the teacher can use in the moment, and frames any class-facing note around what's working.

**Worked example.**
> "Table 3 had everything packed and were standing behind their chairs in under ten seconds — that's exactly the reset I mean."

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* specific, earned, frequent. *Badly:* generic "good job, class," or a ratio that's all corrections.

**Watch out.** Praise has to be specific and true, or students tune it out.

### Lower the friction inside the transition itself

- **Evidence strength:** Moderate · **Source:** channel-factors / behavioral design (Leventhal, Singer & Jones, 1965; see `reducing-missing-work.md`) · **Retrieved:** 2026-06-14

**What the research found.** Behavior follows the path of least friction. The unstructured gap — "we're done, now what?" — is where disruption starts. A visible timer, one clear signal, and a "what to do the second you're done" instruction shrink that gap.

**The move.** The AI builds a countdown timer slide, one signal, and a next-action line.

**Worked example.**
> [Slide: a 2:00 countdown. Header: "Pack up, then start the exit ticket." One chime when it hits zero — that's the only signal.]

**Done well vs. done badly.** *Well:* one clear cue plus a next action to land on. *Badly:* several signals competing, or a blank gap with nothing to do.

**Watch out.** One cue, not five — competing signals are their own kind of noise.

## Handle with care (for this goal)

- **Clip charts / public behavior color-coding.** Public shame, weak evidence, real harm; violates principle 7. Hard no.
- **Whole-class collective punishment.** Penalizes the students who were on task; no support in the research.
- **Vendor behavior programs with their own impact studies.** EEF notes evaluations run by the people selling the program tend to overstate effects — treat vendor numbers skeptically.
