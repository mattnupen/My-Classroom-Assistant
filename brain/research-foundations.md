# Research Foundations

The teaching principles in `brain/teaching-principles.md` aren't made up — they come from decades of education and psychology research. Here's the short version of who did the work, what they found, and where to read more if you're curious.

This isn't required reading. The AI follows the principles whether or not you read this file.

---

| Principle | Research |
|---|---|
| **Wise feedback** — critique wrapped in belief in growth | Cohen, Steele & Ross (1999); Yeager et al. (2014). Showed that the same critique lands very differently depending on whether it's framed as judgment or as a coach who believes the student can hit a higher standard. |
| **Implementation intentions** — turning goals into "if-then" plans | Gollwitzer (1999); Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006). Goals attached to specific triggers are 2-3x more likely to be completed than goals without triggers. |
| **Identity-based motivation** — people work toward goals that feel like part of who they are | Oyserman et al. (2006, 2017). Students perform better when academic work feels connected to their existing identity, not when it requires them to become a different person. |
| **Growth mindset** — praise effort and strategy, not innate ability | Dweck & Mueller (1998); Dweck (2006). Praising "you're so smart" reduces persistence on hard tasks; praising "you worked hard on that" increases it. |
| **Social norming** — describe what most people are doing, not what's wrong | Cialdini & Goldstein (2004); Schultz et al. (2007). "Most students turned this in by Friday" works. "You're behind" backfires. |
| **Social belonging** — small interventions that signal you belong here have outsized effects | Walton & Cohen (2007, 2011). A short message that says "feeling out of place at first is normal — everyone does, and it passes" measurably improves outcomes for marginalized students. |
| **Public celebration, private correction** | A consistent finding across classroom-management research. Public shame predicts disengagement; public celebration predicts belonging. |
| **Small next steps** — tasks that can be started right now, not "catch up by Friday" | Habit and behavior change research generally (Fogg, *Tiny Habits*, 2019). Big asks don't get started. Small asks compound. |

## If you want to read one thing

David Yeager, *The Power of Adolescents* (2024) is the most readable single book that ties most of this together for middle and high school audiences.

For elementary, Carol Dweck's *Mindset* (2006) is still the best entry point — but read the updated 2016 essay where she clarifies what "growth mindset" actually means in classrooms (it's not "say the word 'yet' a lot").

## A note on what isn't proven

The research above is well-replicated. A few things this project does aren't research-backed in the same rigorous way:

- **Co-creating an AI persona with students.** No studies exist (yet). Built on adjacent research about voice and student agency, but the specific intervention is new.
- **Daily prose summaries from teacher to AI.** A workflow choice for privacy reasons, not a proven instructional move.
- **Local browser tools for printable cards.** Tooling, not pedagogy.

If you adapt the project and find something working (or not), share what you learned. The field is small enough that one careful teacher's report matters.
