# How Student Data Is Handled in This Project

*This document is for administrators, district privacy officers, parents who want detail, and any teacher who wants to understand the data design before using the project.*

The short version: **the AI in this project never receives raw student data.** Anything that requires per-student information happens locally on the teacher's laptop, using small browser-based tools. The AI only ever sees broad class-level summaries with no names attached.

This document explains how that works in practice and how it lines up with student data privacy law.

---

## What the AI sees vs. what it doesn't

| The AI **never** sees | The AI **does** see |
|---|---|
| Individual student names | The teacher's own observations of the class ("the class was tired today") |
| Individual grades | Aggregate class summaries ("8 students strong, 12 steady, 4 struggling") |
| Per-student missing-work lists | Counts of which assignments most students are missing |
| Student messages or written work | Themes the teacher synthesizes from anonymous feedback |
| Gradebook exports | Templates with placeholder slots for the local tools to fill in |
| Parent contact info | Generic templates for the teacher to merge with names locally |
| IEP / 504 details | Reminders that some students need different accommodations |

## How the local tools work

The project includes a suite of HTML files in a `local-tools/` folder. The teacher opens them in their web browser like any normal webpage, with one difference: **they run entirely on the teacher's computer**. No network connection is made when the tools process gradebook data.

The teacher drags their gradebook export (`.csv` or `.xlsx`) onto one of the tools. The tool reads the file in the browser, generates whatever the teacher needs (printable cards, certificates, mail-merged messages, etc.), and that's it. The gradebook never leaves the laptop.

The tools include:

- **`ClassAI-dashboard.html`** — the teacher's home base. Shows class goals and progress; links to every other tool. Stays in the browser.
- **`student-cards.html`** — generates per-student printable cards (missing-work or full-progress) for the teacher to hand out.
- **`class-pulse.html`** — generates an aggregate class summary the teacher can safely share with the AI. **This tool's output is explicitly designed to contain no names, no per-student rows, and no information that could be linked back to a specific student.** The teacher can review the output before sharing it.
- **`gradebook-analytics.html`** — a sortable per-student view of the gradebook for the teacher's eyes only. Stays in the browser.
- **`parent-messages.html`** — the teacher writes templates with placeholders; the tool fills them in locally. The merged messages stay in the browser until the teacher copies them out and sends through their normal channel (ParentSquare, email, etc.).
- **`badges.html`, `random-groups.html`** — operate on a small JSON file the teacher keeps on their computer for tracking history. Never uploaded anywhere.

The technical detail: these tools use a JavaScript library called SheetJS (an open-source, widely-used spreadsheet parser). SheetJS runs in the browser. No data is sent over the network as part of processing.

## What the AI receives instead

When the teacher wants the AI's help with class planning, they typically share:

1. **Prose summaries.** "Period 3 had low energy today. Two students made comebacks this week. Tomorrow's assembly will eat 20 minutes. I want a slide that hits the theme of small daily wins."
2. **Aggregate snapshots** generated by `class-pulse.html`. Example:
   ```
   Period A: 24 students
     Strong: 8 · Steady: 12 · Struggling: 4
     Most-missed assignment: "Theme Essay Draft" (6 students)
     This week vs. last: +2 to strong, -1 to struggling
   ```
3. **Templates the teacher wants help refining** — parent message templates, slide outlines, persona instructions.

The AI uses these inputs to produce drafts. The teacher reviews every draft before it reaches a student or family.

## Compliance with FERPA

FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act, 20 U.S.C. § 1232g) governs student education records held by schools. Two relevant points:

1. **Education records are records maintained by the school that contain personally identifiable information (PII).** Aggregate, de-identified data is not an education record under FERPA. The data the AI sees in this project is aggregate and contains no PII.
2. **De-identification standard.** Under 34 CFR § 99.31(b), data must have all direct identifiers removed AND not contain enough indirect identifiers that a reasonable person could re-identify a student. The aggregate summaries this project produces (tier counts, most-missed assignments at the class level, week-over-week movement) meet this standard.

The raw data — gradebook exports — never leaves the teacher's computer. Schools' existing rules about how teachers store gradebook exports on their devices apply unchanged.

## Compliance with state student data privacy laws

Many states have stricter laws than FERPA. Examples:

- **California (SOPIPA / AB 1584):** restricts what third parties can do with student data.
- **Illinois (SOPPA, 105 ILCS 85/):** requires schools to publish lists of approved vendors handling student data and to have signed data privacy agreements.
- **New York (Education Law § 2-d):** requires similar vendor agreements.
- **Colorado, Connecticut, and many others** have similar rules.

Because this project does not transmit student data to any third party (including the AI), these vendor-agreement requirements generally do not apply to the data layer of this project. **However**, administrators should still verify with their district's privacy officer that:

1. Use of AI tools by teachers is permitted under district policy at all.
2. The Claude account the teacher uses for the AI side is set up with appropriate data retention settings (Anthropic offers zero-retention options for educational and enterprise users; check current docs).

If your district's privacy officer wants to verify the data flow themselves, the project is fully transparent — the source code of every local tool is plain HTML/JavaScript and can be inspected.

## Compliance with Anthropic's Usage Policies

Anthropic's Usage Policies place additional restrictions on use cases involving minors. Because students do not interact directly with the AI in this project — and because student data does not flow through the AI — many of these restrictions don't apply directly. But teachers should:

- Review Anthropic's current [Usage Policies](https://www.anthropic.com/legal/aup) before launching
- Use an account that has zero-data-retention enabled, or work through their district's enterprise Anthropic agreement if one exists
- Not use the AI to do anything the policies forbid (no medical/legal/mental-health diagnosis of students, etc. — covered in `brain/safety-rules.md`)

## What happens if a teacher accidentally pastes student data to the AI

The AI is instructed to refuse, gently redirect, and not retain or use the data:

> "I'd rather not work with named student records here — could you run that through `class-pulse.html` first, or summarize what you're seeing in your own words?"

This redirect is documented in `CLAUDE.md` and reinforced in `brain/safety-rules.md`.

## What happens to data over time

- **Local browser tools:** keep nothing after the browser tab closes, except for explicitly saved files the teacher downloads themselves (snapshots, state files, exported templates).
- **AI conversations in Cowork:** subject to Cowork's data handling, which the teacher should review separately.
- **Class state file (`class-state.json`):** lives wherever the teacher saves it. Recommended: outside the project folder, in Documents or Drive. The teacher owns and controls this file.

## If a family opts out

The project's parent letter (in `parent-letter.md`) explicitly offers opt-out. If a family opts out:

- The teacher keeps a private list of opted-out students.
- When running the local tools (which generate per-student materials), the teacher filters those students out and produces their materials manually.
- The student should not be able to tell they're being treated differently — they just don't receive AI-touched content.

## Questions

This is a small, transparent project. There is no company, no terms of service, no data-collection back-end. The teacher who downloaded it is the entire chain of custody.

For questions about the project's design, contact the teacher running the experiment.

For questions about your district's specific compliance posture, consult your district's data privacy officer.
